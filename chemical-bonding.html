Chemical Bonding

Chemical bonding is the process by which atoms combine to form molecules or compounds through the sharing or transfer of electrons. Bonds determine the properties of substances, such as their strength, conductivity, and reactivity. The main types of chemical bonds are ionic, covalent, and metallic.

Ionic Bonding: Ionic bonds form when electrons are transferred from one atom to another, creating oppositely charged ions that attract each other. For example, sodium chloride (NaCl) forms when sodium donates an electron to chlorine. Application: Ionic compounds like salt are used in food preservation due to their stability and solubility.

Covalent Bonding: Covalent bonds involve the sharing of electrons between atoms to achieve stability. For instance, water (H₂O) forms through covalent bonds between oxygen and hydrogen. Application: Covalent compounds like plastics (polymers) are used in manufacturing due to their durability and flexibility.

Metallic Bonding: Metallic bonds occur in metals, where electrons are delocalized, forming a "sea" of electrons that hold metal atoms together. This gives metals their conductivity and malleability. Application: Copper’s metallic bonding makes it an excellent conductor for electrical wiring.
