Acids and Bases

Acids and bases are classes of chemical compounds defined by their behavior in aqueous solutions. Acids donate protons (H⁺), while bases accept protons or donate hydroxide ions (OH⁻). Their properties and interactions, measured by the pH scale, are crucial in chemistry and everyday applications.

Properties of Acids: Acids taste sour, turn blue litmus paper red, and release H⁺ ions in water. Examples include hydrochloric acid (HCl) and citric acid. Application: Citric acid in lemons is used in food and beverages to add flavor and act as a preservative.

Properties of Bases: Bases taste bitter, feel slippery, and turn red litmus paper blue. They release OH⁻ ions in water, like sodium hydroxide (NaOH). Application: Sodium hydroxide is used in soap-making, breaking down fats to form soap through saponification.

pH Scale: The pH scale measures the acidity or basicity of a solution, ranging from 0 (very acidic) to 14 (very basic), with 7 being neutral. Application: In agriculture, soil pH is tested to ensure optimal conditions for crop growth, as most plants thrive in slightly acidic to neutral soils.
