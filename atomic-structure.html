Atomic Structure

Atomic structure refers to the composition and organization of atoms, the fundamental building blocks of matter. Atoms consist of a nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons in specific energy levels. Understanding atomic structure is key to explaining chemical behavior and the properties of elements.

Protons, Neutrons, and Electrons: Atoms are made of protons (positively charged), neutrons (neutral), and electrons (negatively charged). Protons and neutrons reside in the nucleus, while electrons orbit in shells. The number of protons defines the element. Application: In medical imaging, such as PET scans, radioactive atoms emit particles, allowing doctors to visualize internal organs.

Electron Configuration: Electrons occupy specific energy levels and orbitals around the nucleus, following rules like the Aufbau principle. This arrangement determines an element's chemical properties. Application: In spectroscopy, electron transitions between energy levels produce unique light spectra, used to identify elements in stars.

Isotopes: Isotopes are atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons, affecting their mass but not their chemical properties. For example, Carbon-12 and Carbon-14 are isotopes. Application: Carbon-14 dating uses the decay of this isotope to determine the age of ancient artifacts.
